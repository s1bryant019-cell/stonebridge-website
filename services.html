What psychotherapy is.

Psychotherapy is a structured clinical relationship where you meet with a trained clinician to understand what has been difficult, identify patterns, and work toward change over time. It is not about explaining everything perfectly at the start. A good therapy process helps a person feel understood without being reduced to symptoms.

A steady place to make sense of things.

Therapy gives regular time to slow down and look at what is happening in your life, your relationships, your emotions, and your ways of coping. The work may include support, reflection, practical skills, and careful attention to patterns that keep returning.

What happens in therapy?

You and your clinician talk about what brings you in, what you want help with, and what gets in the way. Over time, therapy may help you name emotions more clearly, understand reactions, practice new responses, and relate to yourself and others with more steadiness.

Therapy may feel supportive and clarifying. It can also feel difficult at times, especially when painful experiences, grief, trauma responses, boundaries, or long-standing patterns come into focus.

What people often use therapy for.

People do not need to have the right words before reaching out. Therapy can be useful when distress has become hard to carry alone, when old patterns keep repeating, or when life requires more support, clarity, and care than a person has had available.

Anxiety and worryRacing thoughts, panic, dread, avoidance, or feeling unable to settle.
Low mood or depressionSadness, numbness, loss of interest, shame, self-criticism, or feeling stuck.
Trauma responsesIntrusive memories, emotional shutdown, hypervigilance, or reactions that feel bigger than the moment.
RelationshipsConflict, distance, repeated patterns, boundary problems, or wanting to feel more understood.
Grief and lossDeath, separation, family rupture, health changes, or losses that others may not fully recognize.
Stress and burnoutFeeling depleted, over-responsible, emotionally stretched, or unable to recover between demands.
Teen concernsSchool stress, identity questions, family strain, mood changes, anxiety, or difficulty coping.
Life transitionsChanges in work, school, family, identity, caregiving, independence, or sense of direction.
Substance use concernsQuestions about coping, alcohol or drug use patterns, and whether outpatient therapy is enough support.
Emotion regulationFeeling flooded, reactive, shut down, angry, or unsure how to respond to strong emotion.
Self-understandingWanting to understand why certain patterns, roles, or feelings keep showing up.
Feeling disconnectedFrom yourself, other people, your body, your values, or the life you are trying to build.

This list is not a diagnosis and it is not a promise that Stonebridge is the right fit for every concern. The consultation process helps determine whether outpatient telehealth psychotherapy is clinically appropriate.

What the service includes.

For many people, the question is not only “what is therapy?” It is also “what am I actually receiving?” Stonebridge explains the frame clearly so clients know what the service is before beginning.

Reserved clinical timeA scheduled appointment block held for your care, not a drop-in or message-based service.
Direct therapy timeStandard sessions include 45–50 minutes of direct clinical time within a 60-minute appointment block.
Treatment focusAttention to goals, symptoms, relationships, history, coping, identity, and patterns that matter to your life.
Clinical judgmentOngoing attention to whether the service remains appropriate, ethical, and within outpatient telehealth scope.
DocumentationProfessional recordkeeping that supports continuity, care planning, billing, and ethical practice.
Telehealth structureOnline care for clients located in Illinois, with privacy, safety, and technology expectations discussed before care begins.
Clear boundariesDefined expectations for communication, attendance, cancellations, emergencies, and between-session contact.
Referral honestyIf another provider, specialty, setting, or level of care is more appropriate, Stonebridge will say so respectfully.

How the process works.

Stonebridge uses a consultation-first model because good care starts with fit. The goal is not to rush someone into an appointment. The goal is to understand whether outpatient telehealth psychotherapy is the right kind of care.

Step 1

Send a brief inquiry.

A few sentences are enough. You do not need to explain everything perfectly.

Step 2

Stonebridge reviews fit.

The request is reviewed for scope, outpatient appropriateness, telehealth fit, insurance/payment needs, and next steps.

Step 3

Consultation if appropriate.

A brief consultation may be used to clarify whether Stonebridge may be an appropriate setting. It is not therapy and does not establish a treatment relationship.

Step 4

Intake comes later.

If appropriate and available, intake paperwork, consent forms, and scheduling are coordinated before care begins.

Fit, scope, and services outside this pathway.

Clear boundaries protect clients. The psychotherapy inquiry pathway is for outpatient telehealth therapy. Some needs require a different procedure, a different provider, in-person care, or a higher level of support.

Psychotherapy is the active care pathway.

Stonebridge is currently focused on outpatient telehealth psychotherapy for teens and adults located in Illinois. Assessment and forensic services are part of the broader professional background, but they are not the psychotherapy request pathway.

Assessment and forensic services use dedicated procedures beyond the psychotherapy consultation pathway.

This page is not for every need.

  • Psychotherapy is not an emergency or crisis service. If you are in immediate danger or may harm yourself or someone else, call 911, go to the nearest emergency room, or call/text 988.
  • Psychotherapy is not psychological testing, a court evaluation, a custody opinion, disability documentation, or a legal/forensic report.
  • Outpatient telehealth may not be enough for concerns requiring intensive monitoring, detox, residential care, partial hospitalization, intensive outpatient treatment, or in-person care.

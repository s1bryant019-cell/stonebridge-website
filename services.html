What psychotherapy is.

Psychotherapy is a structured clinical relationship where you meet regularly with a trained clinician to understand what is happening, put words to what has been difficult to carry, and develop more workable ways of living, relating, and coping.

It is more than advice.

Therapy is not simply being told what to do. It is a place to slow down with another person who is listening carefully, noticing patterns, asking thoughtful questions, and helping you understand your experience with more clarity.

Sometimes therapy includes practical tools. Sometimes it focuses on feelings, relationships, history, identity, or repeated patterns. Often it includes both.

It is used for symptoms and for life patterns.

People use therapy when anxiety, depression, trauma responses, grief, stress, relationships, substance use concerns, family conflict, school or work pressure, identity questions, or emotional patterns have become hard to manage alone.

Therapy should help people feel understood without being reduced to symptoms.

Common clinical concerns.

These are examples of common DSM-5-TR diagnostic areas that often bring people to therapy. They are not a self-diagnosis checklist. A diagnosis, when needed, is made through clinical evaluation and held in the context of the whole person.

Anxiety disordersExcessive worry, panic, social anxiety, avoidance, fear, or feeling constantly on edge.
Major depressive disorderPersistent low mood, loss of interest, changes in sleep or appetite, guilt, fatigue, or hopelessness.
Persistent depressive disorderLonger-standing depression or low mood that may feel like part of everyday life.
Trauma- and stressor-related disordersPosttraumatic stress, acute stress, adjustment concerns, or reactions to overwhelming events.
ADHDDifficulty with attention, organization, follow-through, impulsivity, time management, or school/work functioning.
Obsessive-compulsive disorderIntrusive thoughts, compulsive behaviors, checking, reassurance-seeking, or repeated mental loops.
Bipolar-related concernsMood episodes, shifts in energy, sleep changes, irritability, or periods of unusually elevated or activated mood.
Substance use disordersAlcohol, cannabis, or other substance use concerns when outpatient telehealth therapy is clinically appropriate.
Eating and body-image concernsDistress around eating, body image, control, shame, or related emotional patterns, when outpatient care is a responsible fit.
Personality and relational patternsLongstanding patterns with trust, attachment, self-worth, conflict, emotional intensity, or unstable relationships.
Some concerns require psychiatric care, emergency support, intensive outpatient treatment, in-person services, medical monitoring, or another specialty setting. Consultation helps determine whether Stonebridge is an appropriate place to begin.

Stonebridge areas of focus.

Diagnosis can help organize care, but therapy also attends to the human experience underneath the label: what happened, what adapted, what hurts, and what needs to become clearer.

Trauma responses

Working with fear, shame, avoidance, numbness, anger, grief, and patterns shaped by overwhelming experience.

Relationships

Understanding attachment patterns, conflict, loneliness, boundaries, trust, and the wish to feel known more fully.

Self-worth and identity

Making sense of self-criticism, uncertainty, perfectionism, disconnection, identity development, and feeling unseen.

Stress and burnout

Addressing overwhelm, depletion, role strain, work pressure, caregiving burden, and difficulty recovering between demands.

Adolescent concerns

Support for teens navigating anxiety, mood, identity, school pressure, family stress, relationships, and emotional regulation.

Life transitions

Support during loss, change, separation, young adulthood, parenting stress, career shifts, or periods of personal reorganization.

Fit and scope.

Therapy works best when the service, setting, clinician, and client needs fit together. Stonebridge uses consultation to protect that fit before treatment begins.

Telehealth only

Stonebridge provides outpatient psychotherapy by telehealth for clients located in Illinois. Clients need a private, safe place to attend sessions.

Not crisis care

Stonebridge is not an emergency or crisis service. If someone is in immediate danger or cannot stay safe, emergency services, 988, or a local crisis resource should be used.

Assessment and forensic work

Assessment and forensic services are not part of the psychotherapy waitlist pathway. They require dedicated procedures beyond this request process.

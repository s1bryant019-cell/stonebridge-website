Forensic Services

Forensic services approached with structure and care.

Stonebridge offers forensic services with attention to professional structure, psychological clarity, and the distinct demands of evaluative work in more formal or legally relevant contexts.

The nature of forensic work

Forensic services differ from psychotherapy and general clinical assessment. They require a more defined evaluative frame, greater attention to referral questions, and clarity about the purpose and context of the work being requested.

Structured and professionally defined evaluations
Careful attention to referral questions and scope
Clear boundaries between treatment and evaluation roles

What may be considered

Forensic inquiries may involve requests for assessment, consultation, record review, or more specialized evaluative services depending on the nature of the case and the specific referral question.

Why consultation matters first

Because forensic work depends so heavily on context, role, and scope, the initial inquiry is especially important. It helps determine whether the requested service is appropriate for the practice and whether the matter fits the intended frame of evaluation.

Professional fit and referrals

Not all forensic requests are appropriate for every setting. If a request is outside the intended scope of the practice, thoughtful referral guidance may still be offered when useful.

Beginning a forensic inquiry

The consultation request form can be used to describe the broad nature of the forensic matter, the type of service being sought, and any relevant context. That first step helps determine whether the inquiry appears appropriate for Stonebridge and what next steps may follow.

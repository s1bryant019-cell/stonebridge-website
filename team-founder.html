PsyD, LCPC · Founder · Doctoral-level clinician

Dr. Bryant provides online psychotherapy for teens and adults in Illinois, with particular depth in addiction, trauma, mood concerns, and complex co-occurring patterns. Parent consultation is also available.

Clinical breadth

Areas of depth and broader care.

Dr. Bryant works with a wide range of concerns. His strongest clinical depth is in addiction and co-occurring disorders, trauma and complex stress, and mood and anxiety conditions.

The areas below are not a complete list. Other concerns may be considered when outpatient telehealth is clinically appropriate and within scope.

Addiction and co-occurring concerns

Substance use, recovery, shame, and dual-diagnosis patterns

For substance use concerns, relapse patterns, ambivalence, process addictions, coping cycles, and co-occurring emotional distress when outpatient therapy is appropriate.

Trauma, PTSD, and complex stress

Threat responses, safety, dissociation, and old protections

For clients whose earlier experiences still shape trust, self-worth, emotional regulation, body-based distress, or the ways they relate to themselves and others.

Mood and anxiety conditions

Depression, panic, irritability, shutdown, and emotional instability

For worry, overthinking, avoidance, numbness, hopelessness, mood shifts, and difficulty feeling steady in daily life.

Broader concerns and consultation

Relationships, identity, grief, neurodevelopmental concerns, and family stress

Other work may include self-worth, attachment and relationship patterns, life transitions, emotional regulation, ADHD or autism-related concerns, and parent consultation when clinically appropriate.

Therapy style

A steady, relational style.

The work often slows things down enough for patterns to become visible.

Sessions may look at what is happening now and how earlier experiences, relationships, losses, coping strategies, and protective patterns still shape life. The pace is collaborative and attentive to readiness, safety, and the client’s goals.

Relational The therapy relationship is treated as part of the work, not just the container for it.
Insight-oriented Patterns, feelings, defenses, needs, and meanings are explored so experience becomes more understandable.
Practical when useful CBT, DBT, ACT, motivational interviewing, and mindfulness-informed tools may be integrated when they help.

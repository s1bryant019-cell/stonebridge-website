How I work

Therapy with Dr. Bryant is primarily insight-oriented. The work looks beneath immediate symptoms to understand recurring emotional patterns, relationship templates, losses, conflicts, defenses, and ways of coping that may have once been protective but now feel limiting.

His approach is grounded in psychodynamic, relational, trauma-informed, and self-psychological therapy. Practical tools from CBT, DBT, ACT, motivational interviewing, and mindfulness may be integrated when structure, coping skills, behavior change, or emotional regulation would support the deeper work.

The goal is not quick advice or generic reassurance. The goal is to help clients understand themselves more clearly, feel less controlled by old patterns, and develop more flexible ways of relating to themselves and others.

What therapy may look like

Sessions are conversational, reflective, and active. You may begin with something happening now — anxiety, grief, conflict, substance use concerns, burnout, trauma reminders, or feeling disconnected from yourself — but the work often asks what is underneath the immediate problem.

Together, you may slow down a reaction, conflict, urge, or emotional shift and ask: Why does this keep happening? What does this protect? What feels difficult to say directly?

Some sessions may feel practical and focused on current life. Others may move more deeply into history, identity, shame, loss, attachment, or patterns that have been difficult to understand alone.

Professional background

Dr. Bryant is a doctoral-level clinician and Licensed Clinical Professional Counselor with training in clinical psychology, counseling, and forensic psychology. His background includes outpatient private practice, community mental health, intensive outpatient treatment, partial hospitalization, inpatient behavioral health, psychological assessment, supervision, and interdisciplinary treatment planning.

His broader professional work includes trauma-focused treatment, substance use care, psychological assessment, supervision of doctoral-level trainees, professional education, and research on clinician burnout. At Stonebridge, his current clinical work is focused on outpatient telehealth psychotherapy for teens and adults located in Illinois.

Before reaching out

Stonebridge provides outpatient telehealth psychotherapy. A brief consultation helps determine whether the practice is an appropriate fit for the client’s needs, location, age, risk level, and scheduling needs.

Some concerns may be better supported by a higher level of care, in-person treatment, emergency support, psychological testing, forensic evaluation, or a specialized treatment program. If another setting would be more appropriate, referral guidance can be offered when possible.
